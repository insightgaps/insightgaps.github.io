01 — The Scale

A generation being
poisoned in plain sight

The 2025 UNICEF-Bangladesh Bureau of Statistics Multiple Indicator Cluster Survey is the most comprehensive national measurement of childhood lead exposure ever conducted in Bangladesh. Its finding is unambiguous: 38% of children aged 12–59 months carry blood lead levels above the internationally recognised safe threshold. In Dhaka, that figure reaches 65%.

Lead causes irreversible neurological damage. There is no cure. The damage — measured in lost IQ points, impaired physical development, and lifelong cardiovascular risk — cannot be undone after exposure. Every day that the sources continue to operate is another day of permanent harm to children who cannot protect themselves.

Bangladesh now ranks 4th globally by total number of children affected by lead pollution. The annual economic cost, measured only in lost productivity from IQ reduction, is USD 15.9 billion.

02 — The Source

6,000 illegal recyclers.
6 are registered.

The primary driver of lead contamination in Bangladesh is the informal used lead-acid battery recycling sector. When a vehicle battery dies, it enters a supply chain that ends — for the vast majority — at an unregistered backyard smelter where the battery is broken open, the lead melted in open air, and the toxic residue left to leach into soil, water, and the lungs of nearby residents.

Registration gap — formal vs informal ULAB recycling operations

Informal / unregistered operations (ESDO estimate)6,000+
Formally registered operators (national)6

Six formally registered battery recyclers exist in all of Bangladesh. The rest — estimated at over 6,000 operations by the Environment and Social Development Organisation in collaboration with UNEP — operate without registration, without environmental clearance, without health oversight, and without any requirement to safely dispose of toxic byproducts.

Pure Earth, working with the University of Dhaka's Geology Department, has assessed 270 specific contaminated sites across the country. Their data documents lead concentrations far exceeding international safety standards — at levels that cause measurable developmental harm in children living nearby.

Children living within 200 metres of one assessed site in Savar — the Kathgora location — had median blood lead levels of 21.3 micrograms per decilitre. That is more than four times the WHO reference level. At a site that had already stopped operating.
Source: icddr,b / Stanford University study, Environmental Research, 2021 — BD-KATH, Ashulia, Savar

04 — The Regulatory Failure

They knew.
They did not act.

The regulatory architecture exists. Bangladesh's Environment Conservation Act requires Environmental Clearance Certificates for industrial operations including battery recycling. The Department of Environment has authority to inspect, fine, and close non-compliant operations. The Department of Inspection for Factories and Establishments has jurisdiction over worker safety. The legal tools are present. The institutions are present. The crisis is present. What is absent is enforcement.

Bangladesh Bureau of Statistics baseline: 12,207 battery recycling operations nationally — 34% in or near Dhaka
Data established. Regulatory action documented: none.
2009–2012
First major academic documentation of ULAB lead contamination in Dhaka
Dholaikhal, Doyagonj, Sadarghat identified as primary contamination zones. Soil lead far above international limits. Operations continue.
2016
Geli Industrial Company Ltd, Sreepur, Gazipur — begins operations without Environmental Clearance Certificate
DoE made aware of non-compliance. A school established since 1968 operates nearby. The factory continues to run.
2019–2020
Pure Earth and University of Dhaka complete assessment of 270+ contaminated sites nationally
Data submitted to international databases. High contamination confirmed across assessed sites. No reported permanent closures follow.
2021
icddr,b and Stanford measure children's blood lead near Kathgora, Savar — median 21.3 μg/dL, 4.3× reference level
The site had already stopped operating. Children still poisoned from residual soil contamination. Blood lead remained elevated seven months after soil remediation.
November 2024
UNICEF and Ministry of Environment jointly host lead pollution workshop in Dhaka
Government acknowledges the crisis. UNICEF calls for partners to help identify sources — despite sources having been mapped and published since 2019.
November 2025
MICS 2025 confirms: 38% of Bangladesh's children, 65% of Dhaka's children — above safe blood lead levels
The most comprehensive measurement to date. The crisis has not improved. The informal recycling sector continues to operate at scale.
March 2026 — This Investigation
Insight Gaps spatial analysis: 44 sites, 145 schools, 58,000 students within 500 metres
Original finding. Never before published. Three sites satellite-confirmed active by Gemini Vision AI. No site on record as permanently closed.
Over two decades of documented contamination. Six thousand illegal operators. Two hundred and seventy assessed sites. A national blood lead crisis affecting every third child. The regulatory agencies have the authority. They have the evidence. What Bangladesh's children have received in return is another report, another workshop, another year of exposure.

05 — The Economic Argument

A $15.9 billion
annual governance failure

Lead poisoning is not only a public health tragedy. It is a governance failure with a measurable price tag. The annual productivity loss from IQ reduction caused by childhood lead exposure in Bangladesh is estimated at USD 15.9 billion. This covers only the most easily quantifiable pathway of harm. It excludes healthcare costs, premature deaths linked to cardiovascular effects of lifetime lead exposure, and the USD 91 million in annual tax revenue foregone from the unregulated, untaxed informal recycling sector.

The cost of enforcement — inspection staffing, closure orders, site remediation — would be a fraction of this figure. The political will has not materialised across any government in the past two decades.

06 — Methodology & Transparency

How this
investigation
was built

This investigation is based entirely on publicly available data and original spatial analysis conducted by Insight Gaps. No RTI requests are pending. Every figure cited can be independently verified from the original sources listed below. The proximity analysis methodology — Pure Earth site coordinates + OpenStreetMap school locations + Haversine distance calculation — can be replicated by any data journalist using freely available tools.

The primary original finding — 44 assessed lead-contaminated sites with schools within 500 metres, placing an estimated 58,000 students in proximity — has not been previously published. It is the product of Insight Gaps' own analysis.

Satellite imagery for the three highest-priority sites (BD-4591, BD-4653, BD-4802) was retrieved and submitted to Google Gemini Vision AI for structured analysis in March 2026. All three were assessed as showing active industrial operations with residential and educational buildings in proximity.

On the 58,000 students estimate: This figure was calculated by multiplying the count of school buildings within 500 metres of each assessed site (145 schools) by the BANBEIS national average primary school enrollment of 400 students. This is a conservative estimate — some schools in the dataset are secondary or higher institutions with larger enrollments. The figure should be read as a floor, not a ceiling.

On the enforcement gap claim: The statement that no ULAB facility has been permanently closed by regulatory action is based on absence of any such record in the Pure Earth database (which tracks site status), archived news coverage 2010–2025, ESDO published reporting, and UNEP assessment documentation. Insight Gaps welcomes correction from DoE or DIFE with documented evidence of permanent closures.
