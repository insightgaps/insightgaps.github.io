Her Story

She did the calculation before she told anyone. She ran it alone, in the dark, the night it happened.

If she spoke: her family would find out. Her family finding out meant her marriage prospects — her mother's planning, her father's standing in the neighbourhood — were gone. There would be a shalish, local elders deciding the matter over her head, a settlement, the man paying money to her family as though this were a transaction she had a stake in. 62 to 64 per cent of women in Bangladesh made the same calculation. The system taught them the answer.

Her Story

A friend told her about Runa Akhter. She had no idea who Runa Akhter was before that day. Her friend said: there was a woman in Cox's Bazar who filed a case. Then they filed a case against her. She was convicted in 56 days. Her case is still waiting.

Her Story

She lives in Barishal. She did not choose Barishal. She was born there. Her mother was born there. The violence rate in her division is the highest in the country: 81.5 per cent of women have experienced it in their lifetime.

There is one One-Stop Crisis Centre for her entire division. Nine million people. One centre. It is in the divisional city. She does not live in the divisional city. She has never been to the divisional city. The bus takes four hours and costs money she does not easily have.

The system that was supposed to help her is 4 hours and a bus fare away. For 50 of Bangladesh's 64 districts, it is further than that, or it does not exist at all.

Her Story

She arrived at the hospital eleven days after. The bus had taken four hours. She had waited three days to decide, then waited again, then taken a loan from a cousin to cover the bus fare and the possibility of tests.

The nurse looked at her the way people look when they are about to say something they know will hurt. She said: we cannot help you now. The window is 72 hours. Your evidence is gone.

Neither of them knew that Section 32 of the Women and Children Repression Prevention Act makes it a criminal offence for a hospital to refuse examination of a rape survivor. No institution tracks whether hospitals comply. The law existed. The compliance did not.

She went home on the four-hour bus with her evidence gone, her loan still owed, and a case number that was already weaker than when she had filed it.

Scene 08 · The Paradox

Maximum Severity. Near-Zero Certainty.

Criminological consensus: certainty deters crime. Severity alone does not. Bangladesh chose severity. Watch where it places them.

Nagin 2013 · Dölling 2009 · Abramovaite 2022 · NPA 2023/24 · CPS 2024 · NCRB 2022 · OCC 2024
Step 01 of 03

Criminological consensus: certainty is the primary deterrent.

Nagin (2013): certainty of apprehension — not severity of punishment — is the primary deterrent to crime. Dölling et al. (2009) meta-analysis: deterrence hypothesis “rarely confirmed” for serious offences. This is not a minority view. It is the 50-year consensus across jurisdictions.

Academic consensus · Cross-jurisdictional · No credible counter-evidence for rape specifically
Step 02 of 03

Countries with higher certainty achieve higher conviction rates.

South Africa: high certainty (forensic infrastructure, integrated support) → 72.2% conviction rate. No death penalty for rape. Indonesia: implemented death penalty for child rape → documented post-implementation surge in child rape rates. The mechanism is known. Certainty deters. Severity alone does not.

Susila 2019 · S-52 · CONFIRMED
Step 03 of 03

Bangladesh is in the worst possible corner.

Maximum severity: mandatory death penalty since October 2020. Near-zero certainty: 0.46% conviction rate. The data places Bangladesh exactly where the literature predicts. The reform pathway requires no new theory. It requires political decision. Six evidence-based interventions. All documented below.

Three documented mechanisms: judicial reluctance when penalty is death · witness elimination incentive · family suppression when accused is known
