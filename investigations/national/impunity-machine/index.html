Enter the record

She is somewhere in Bangladesh right now.

She is in her twenties. She lives in Barishal Division — where 81.5 percent of women will experience violence in their lifetimes, the highest rate in the country. Something happened to her three weeks ago. She has not washed the memory away. She has not told her mother. Not her sister. Not a doctor. Not a single person in the world knows what happened to her.

This is not weakness. This is not weakness. She has done a precise calculation — the kind every woman in her position does — and she has concluded, correctly, that speaking will cost her more than silence. Her reputation. Her marriage prospects. Her family's honour, which in Bangladesh is stored, still, in her body. The risk of a shalish. The risk of being the one who ends up in prison.

In Bangladesh, 62 to 64 percent of women who experience sexual violence tell no one at all. Not one person. That silence is the first verdict the system delivers — before any court has heard a word.

"This report's central finding is not that Bangladesh's rape justice system fails.
It is that the system produces impunity with the consistency of a machine.
The silence, the delay, the attrition, the retaliation — these are not accidents.
They are the documented, predictable outputs of identifiable structural choices."

EDITORIAL NOTE: The individual above is a documented composite. All statistics are primary-sourced. No individual's identity is assumed. Composite method is standard in data journalism (ProPublica, NYT, Reuters) and is disclosed here. OCC conviction data (310 of 66,711) sourced to Daily Star, November 2, 2024 (S-55). Silence figure from UNFPA/BBS VAW Survey 2024 (S-02).

She decided to speak. She found the One-Stop Crisis Centre after two days of searching — in a city she had to travel to, because her district has a tribunal but no full OCC. A counsellor held her hand. A doctor examined her. A case was filed.

She is now a number. Case 227 of 643 in the first seven months of 2024.

She does not know that of the 12 verdicts delivered that year, zero ended in conviction. She does not know that her case will sit in a queue for an average of 2,349 days. She does not know yet what the system does to women who wait.

§01

The Conviction Gap

The annual WCRPA conviction rate is not published by PHQ, the Ministry of Law, or the Ministry of Women and Children Affairs. Five independent measures exist. They all say the same thing.

The Convergence Finding

Eight independent sources — different methodologies, time periods, geographic scopes — all place the WCRPA conviction rate below 4%. The sources had no access to each other's data. The convergence is the finding. Even the government's own police headquarters data confirms it.

Why Did 2022 Drop 25%? Three Documented Causes

(1) DNA lab crisis: May 2022 — 54 scientific officers across 8 national labs went unpaid during administrative transition. Reports stalled; cases couldn't proceed. (2) 72-hour rule: Nov 2021 judge recommended police refuse cases filed more than 72 hours post-incident. Though overturned, the chilling effect lasted into 2022. (3) Death penalty paradox: Mandatory death sentence creates intense community pressure to settle by shalish when the perpetrator is a family member or known person.

Why Delay Produces Acquittals — The Mechanism

Delay is not a neutral inconvenience. It is a mechanism of acquittal. Over 2,349 days: witnesses relocate or forget critical details. Forensic evidence degrades beyond usefulness. Economic pressure on the survivor's family becomes unbearable. Shalish threats intensify as trial approaches. The Islam & Islam forensic study directly links delayed forensic attendance to negative forensic opinions in 248 cases (S-50). And where witnesses might have appeared: in some districts, more than 60% of cases collapse simply because key witnesses do not show up. PROBABLE · Bangladesh_Rape_Prosecution_Statistics.docx

"When a case drags on for eight or ten years, doctors retire, complainants lose the will to continue." — Public prosecutor on record · Daily Star 2025 (S-07)
The Magura Exception — What Speed Requires CONFIRMED · S-60, S-67

March 5–6, 2025: eight-year-old Achia raped and murdered in Magura. Nationwide protests. HC directive. Special Public Prosecutor at Attorney General rank. DNA processed in five days. Death sentence: May 17, 2025. 71 days from assault to verdict. Three co-accused acquitted for insufficient evidence. As of March 2026: appeal admitted July 1, 2025 (Justice Mostafizur Rahman + Justice Sagir Hossain). Fine of BDT 100,000 stayed. Appeal under active review. Normal WCRPA timeline: 2,349 days. The system is not incapable of speed. It reserves speed for cases where public outrage makes delay politically impossible.

Legal Architecture — What the Law Says vs. What Happens

Bangladesh enacted the Women and Children Repression Prevention Act in 2000. The 2025 Amendment Ordinance is the most significant reform since. Both reflect the same pattern: legislative ambition, implementation collapse.

2025 WCRPA Amendment Ordinance — March 20, 2025 CONFIRMED · S-21, S-68

The most significant reform in the Act's 25-year history. Law Adviser Asif Nazrul announced the ordinance on March 20, 2025, citing the Magura trial as proof that timely justice is possible. It is a genuine reform — not rhetorical. Whether it survives political transition and whether tribunals receive the staffing to implement it: those are the next questions.

§04

Geographic Distribution

Division, district, economic class, and access to OCC infrastructure determine whether an assault produces evidence, whether that evidence survives the system, and whether a trial ever happens.

The rate is highest in Barishal — 81.5 percent. That means in a room of ten women in Barishal, eight have experienced intimate partner violence. Not "women in difficult circumstances." Not a marginal population. Eight out of ten women in a room. And there is one full One-Stop Crisis Centre in the entire division.

These numbers have names. They have districts. They have specific hospitals where forensic examiners are unavailable at night, and specific courts where the case will wait three years for a charge to be framed, and specific villages where the shalish will meet before the police are ever called. Geography is not context. In Bangladesh's rape justice system, geography is verdict.

§05

What Survivors Face

The path from assault to justice requires navigating multiple hostile institutions within a shrinking forensic window. The first barrier is not the police station. It is the hospital door — which may be closed before she ever gets there.

The Section 32 Catch-22 — The Door That Doesn't Open PROBABLE · S-72

Section 32 of the WCRPA 2000 requires hospitals to examine rape survivors. But in practice, many hospitals across Bangladesh refuse to conduct the examination unless the survivor first presents a police case number. This creates a structural impossibility: you cannot build a forensic case without a medical examination, and you cannot get a medical examination without a case number.

The HRCBM Public Interest Litigation (August 2025) specifically documents this violation. The hospital refusal destroys forensic evidence before the funnel even begins — before a police complaint, before an FIR, before a lawyer. The survivor is turned away, the evidence window closes, and the system never registers that she came at all. PROBABLE · S-72 (HRCBM PIL Aug 2025)

The Shalish — Village Mediation as Impunity Mechanism

The most common response to sexual violence is not a police report. It is a shalish — an informal village tribunal, almost always of men, resolving disputes through compensation and community pressure. The shalish removes the assault from the formal legal system, produces no criminal record, and creates intense pressure on the survivor to agree. The death penalty for rape has made the shalish more attractive: when reporting means a possible execution of a family member, settlement becomes rational. A 2020 High Court directive ordered police to stop recognising shalish settlements in rape cases. Shalish continues in practice.

The Invisible Dimension — Indigenous and Minority Women CONFIRMED · S-88, S-71

SHARE-Net Bangladesh (June 2025) documented 17 rape cases involving indigenous women in the Chittagong Hill Tracts (CHT). Only 5 of those 17 were ever filed with police — a 29.4% filing rate, already lower than the national average of 31%. The remaining 70.6% went entirely unrecorded, erased before the funnel even begins. The barriers compound: geographic isolation, no OCC in CHT, language barriers with investigating officers, documented distrust of state institutions among Hill Tracts communities, and the absence of any minority-sensitive safe shelter. PHQ's 2025 minority incident report documented 58 rape cases classified as "non-communal" — a categorisation that makes the intersectional vulnerability of minority women structurally invisible in national statistics. The South Africa Comparator
South Africa's Thuthuzela Care Centres — integrated medical, forensic, psychological, legal support in one location — documented 78% conviction rate (S-40). Compare Bangladesh's 0.46% for OCC visitors. The gap is not a cultural inevitability. It is a resource and design choice. The OCC model already exists in Bangladesh. It is simply not integrated into the prosecutorial chain.
§07

Deterrence Theory — Why Maximum Severity Fails

The 2020 death penalty amendment was presented as deterrence. Academic literature is unambiguous. South Asian empirical record is unambiguous. Bangladesh post-2020 data is unambiguous.

The Core Proposition

Deterrence theory: crime is deterred by certainty, severity, and celerity. Academic consensus: certainty is dominant. Severity has limited additional effect once certainty is established — and no significant effect when certainty is near zero. Bangladesh has maximum severity (mandatory death penalty) and near-zero certainty (0.39–3.66%). This is the worst possible combination — and the evidence shows three specific, predictable harms.

Mechanism 1 — Judicial Reluctance PROBABLE
Where death is mandatory, judges require near-absolute certainty to convict.
Contested forensic evidence — already degraded by delay — resolves toward acquittal when the alternative is executing a possibly innocent man. Death penalty raises the evidence threshold Bangladesh's system cannot clear.
Mechanism 2 — Witness Elimination PROBABLE
Death penalty gives perpetrators rational incentive to murder the survivor.
If the penalty is death, eliminating the primary witness reduces conviction probability. This perverse incentive is absent when the penalty is imprisonment. Bangladesh has no witness protection law.
Mechanism 3 — Family Suppression PROBABLE
When accused is known to family, death penalty exposure suppresses reporting.
Most perpetrators are known to the victim. When reporting means potentially executing a brother-in-law or neighbour, families intensify pressure to settle through shalish.
Dölling et al. (2009) Meta-Analysis · S-48
Deterrence hypothesis "rarely confirmed" for serious offences including rape.
Comprehensive meta-analysis. Directly undermines severity-only reform arguments.
Susila (2019) Indonesia · S-52

Empirical comparator for Bangladesh 2020. Same mechanism, same failure. India (MP): highest death sentences for rape 2016–2020, highest reporting state in 2022.
South Africa TCC · S-40, S-51
Victim-centric evidence model: 78% conviction. No death penalty.
The alternative: certainty through better evidence quality, not severity of punishment. Works. Documented.

§09

Evidence-Based Interventions

Each intervention is paired with evidence base and documented structural barrier. Where the 2025 Ordinance made partial progress, it is noted.

I-01

Publish annual WCRPA conviction rates by tribunal and district

PHQ holds this data. A single policy decision would make it public. UK MOJ and India NCRB publish comparable data. The absence is a choice. CONFIRMED absent · CONFIRMED solvable

I-02

Enact Witness Protection Legislation — with enforcement budget

Law Commission draft bill exists. Not enacted. India (2018), Sri Lanka (2015), Pakistan (2013/17) have enacted comparable laws. Bangladesh is the regional outlier. Legislation alone insufficient without enforcement budget. CONFIRMED gap · S-43, S-44

I-03

Restore judicial discretion — amend mandatory death penalty

Expert consensus (HRW, Amnesty, OHCHR): mandatory capital punishment raises conviction threshold without improving deterrence. Three documented mechanisms of harm. Certainty more effective than severity (Bailey 1977). Political barrier HIGH. PROBABLE effect

I-04

Enforce the trial timeline — with judicial accountability

180-day rule: compliance zero. 2025 update: 90-day mandate with mandatory disciplinary proceedings for official negligence now enacted. First enforcement mechanism ever attached to this rule in 25 years. Implementation monitoring is the next accountability task. CONFIRMED partial progress · S-21, S-68

I-05

Decentralize forensic medical capacity — expand OCCs and fund DNA labs

2 national DNA machines serve the entire country. 92% of samples unprocessed in early 2025. 2025 update: Ordinance now allows trial without DNA report if medical evidence is sufficient. Removes DNA as absolute barrier. Does not rebuild the labs.

The National Forensic DNA Profiling Laboratory (NFDPL) has existed since 2006 — nearly 20 years. In that time it evaluated 375,982 samples across 11,501 cases but issued reports on only 55% of them. A Faridpur regional screening lab documented sending 76 cases to NFDPL; only 55 reports came back. A 2026 government plan to upgrade all District Scientific Support Laboratories (DSSLs) to independent DNA profiling centres was announced — but this is the same problem identified in 2022 being re-announced, not solved. South Africa TCC (78% vs 0.46%) proves forensic access determines outcomes. PROBABLE · S-66, S-22 · Master Evidence File FORENSIC_CAPACITY

I-06

Criminalize marital rape — amend Penal Code Section 375

CEDAW 2016 para 19(a) explicitly required this (S-41). Nepal criminalized 2017. 2025 Ordinance expanded rape definition but did not address marital exemption. 75.9% lifetime IPV — predominantly from spouses. The majority of sexual violence is legally invisible. CONFIRMED gap · International obligation unfulfilled

I-07

Establish centralized Section 17 counter-prosecution tracking

No institution tracks §17 prosecutions nationally. Two confirmed cases documented. National scale: unknown. Mandatory tracking would quantify the chilling effect — prerequisite for any evidence-based reform. CONFIRMED absent · CONFIRMED actionable

⚠ I-08

Monitor 2025 Ordinance Section 17 "dignity" expansion for chilling effect

The 2025 Ordinance expanded §17 grounds to include "loss of dignity/reputation." This creates a new pathway for defendants to prosecute survivors whose cases are found unproven. The documented pattern (CX-2023-001, BS-2026-001) shows the mechanism already exists. The question is whether the expanded grounds accelerate it. Automated collector will flag all new §17 filings. PROBABLE risk · Active monitoring required · S-68

She filed in October 2024. The political transition had just happened. People were saying things would change. She believed them. She was from a Hindu family in a southern district, and what had happened to her had happened partly because of who her family was. She knew this. The men who did it knew this. The local police who filed her complaint seemed to know this too — the way they wrote it down, carefully not naming what it was.

She does not know yet what the statistics will say about women like her in the months after the revolution. She knows only that it is March 2026, and no one has called about her case in four months.

Conclusion

What the System Produces

She filed her case. She waited. The 180-day statutory deadline passed and no court flagged it. A witness moved to Dhaka. Her forensic evidence — collected 11 days after the assault, well outside the 72-hour window because no full OCC existed in her district — returned an inconclusive DNA result after a four-month wait. Her accused's lawyer filed for continuance. Her family asked her, quietly, to stop.

She is, statistically, in the 98.54% of women who come forward to a crisis centre and leave without a conviction. She did everything the system asked of her. The system did not do what she asked of it.

The Mechanism of Impunity — Documented, Predictable, Structural
Assault occurs→ 64% stay silent→ Shalish intercepts→ Forensic window missed→ FIR registered (if lucky)→ 41% closed unproven→ 2,349 days waiting→ Witnesses gone→ Evidence degraded→ Acquittal

This is not a chain of accidents. Every link is documented. Every failure point has a name, a source, and a tier label. The silence is documented. The shalish is documented. The Section 32 catch-22 is documented. The forensic window failure is documented. The delay is documented. The acquittal is documented. The only thing that is not documented is the government's plan to change any of it at systemic scale.

In March 2025, the interim government enacted the WCRPA Amendment Ordinance — the most significant reform in the law's 25-year history. Genuine reforms. But in the same year that the Ordinance was signed, BMP recorded a 52% rise in documented rape victims. Child rape surged 75% in the first half of the year alone. The reform and the surge are happening simultaneously, in the same country, at the same time. The machine does not stop running because the legislature is busy.

The Magura exception proves the system can move fast when forced to. The Runa Akhter inversion proves the system can convict when power demands it. The Sherpur "technical grounds" dismissal proves the system can protect men with political connections. The Jamalpur 21-year case proves the system can make a woman wait a lifetime. It reserves all of these outcomes for the right circumstances. The question is who gets to be the right circumstance.

The 2025 Ordinance poses one test: can the law force that engine to run for all 150,000 pending cases — or only for the ones that go viral? As of March 2026, we do not know. Because the government does not publish the data that would answer the question.

She withdrew her case on a Tuesday.

She told no one. She came home, sat with her mother, and did not explain. There was nothing to explain. She had tried the system the system had built for her — she had navigated the shalish, found the OCC, waited at the hospital door, survived the forensic examination, named her attacker in a document she signed, and sat through three years of hearings while her witness went silent and her evidence aged in a lab — and the system had done exactly what the data said it would do.

She is not a failure of courage. She is a documented output.

And there are 300,000 more like her this year alone — most of whom will never even reach the door of a crisis centre, because they already know, from watching women like her, what waits on the other side. In 2025, 749 women and children were tracked by ASK. 786 by BMP. 306 children in the first six months. 106 children in March alone.

The machine runs. The numbers accumulate. The silence continues. Somewhere in Bangladesh right now, another woman is doing the calculation. And the system — 150,000 cases deep, 12,000 more every year, 2,349 days average — is waiting patiently for her to decide it isn't worth it.
